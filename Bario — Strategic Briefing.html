Building Bario — the case, the risks, and the path

A structured overview of the Bario business model: what it is, where it breaks, what the economics look like, and how to build something defensible. Prepared for team review.

Model: Bar food ordering platform Primary market: Netherlands → Belgium → Europe ARR target: €1M (NL) as first milestone Status: Pre-pilot
01
What we're building
The Bario concept

Bario connects bars and cafés that have no kitchen to nearby restaurants through a simple, structured ordering flow — letting guests order dinner without leaving the venue, and letting bars increase dwell time and drink revenue without running a kitchen.

For bars & cafés
Increase guest dwell time and drink revenue by offering food without kitchen complexity or staffing cost.
For guests
Order dinner without leaving the venue. Scan a QR code, choose a time slot, food arrives at the bar.
For restaurants
Generate incremental orders during off-peak hours with predictable, batched demand and no marketing cost.
How it works
Step 1
QR code on the table
Physical menu cards with a QR code sit on every table in the bar. No app download required.
Step 2
Guest selects a time slot
Predefined delivery slots (18:00, 19:00, 20:00, 21:00) — each linked to a selected nearby restaurant.
Step 3
Orders are batched
Orders are aggregated per slot and sent to the restaurant as a single batch — predictable, efficient.
Step 4
Delivery to the bar
Meals are delivered at fixed times. Bar staff distribute to guests by name or identifier.
Parent company context: Bario is a spin-off of Mealtime, a platform that simplifies group meal ordering by connecting organisers with multiple food suppliers. Bario applies the same coordination logic to the bar occasion specifically.

02
Where it breaks
Stress test — the hard questions

These are the questions an investor would push on, and the honest answers. Expand each to see the risk and proposed resolution.

Unit economics are brutally thin at base assumptions
Critical
+
The problem: At €2.00/guest and 30 guests/week, each venue generates ~€258/month. You need 325 venues just to reach €1M ARR — before any ops, tech, or marketing cost. The resolution: Two levers together fix it — 55 guests/week + €14 avg spend pushes each venue to €530+/month, cutting the venue count needed nearly in half. Target bars where the average guest already spends €14+ (cocktail bars, premium cafés) rather than student venues.
You don't control the critical experience
Critical
+
The problem: The meal arrives at the bar. Bar staff distribute it. The restaurant cooked it. You own the QR code. If food is late, cold, or wrong — the guest blames the experience and the bar blames Bario. You have operational responsibility but no operational control. The resolution: Contractual SLAs with restaurants (late by >15min = partial refund from restaurant). A reliability score per restaurant visible to you. Guest feedback after every order — one question. Restaurants below threshold get replaced, not managed.
The bar has no skin in the game
Important
+
The problem: Bars don't pay you and don't actively sell. They put out a card and that's it. No financial incentive means passive participation, invisible menu cards, and zero-cost churn. The resolution: Revenue share of €0.50–1.00 per meal turns bars from passive hosts into active promoters. Combined with a SaaS fee (€49–79/month), bars have both an incentive and a commitment. Switching cost goes from zero to meaningful.
The time slot model creates abandonment
Important
+
The problem: Someone who arrives at 19:15 just missed the slot and waits until 20:00 — or leaves. In a social setting, one frustrated experience spreads quickly. The resolution: Rolling slot model: order by :45 past the hour, delivery at the next full hour. Preserves the batching model while eliminating the hard cutoff. Test this with a Typeform edit before building any product.
Density is a prerequisite, not an outcome
Important
+
The problem: The model only works if there's a nearby restaurant willing to partner, in a neighbourhood with enough bars to make restaurant partnerships worthwhile, with enough guests per venue to justify coordination. A three-sided cold start problem. The resolution: Start in one hyper-dense neighbourhood. Amsterdam's Jordaan, Pijp, or Leidseplein areas have the bar density and walkability required. Get 8–10 bars and 2 restaurants operational within 400m before considering anywhere else.
Differentiation is thin once it works
Important
+
The problem: If the model gains traction, what stops Thuisbezorgd from launching a "bar delivery" vertical? They have restaurant relationships, consumer apps, and delivery infrastructure. The resolution: See Section 06 — Defensibility. The short version: exclusivity contracts with bars (signed before you're visible enough to attract platform attention), demand data that compounds with every order, and a POS integration that makes Bario operational infrastructure rather than a feature layer.

The four levers that matter: guests per venue per week (highest impact), average spend (drives ARPU most efficiently), commission rate (ceiling set by restaurant margins), and the SaaS floor fee (de-risks volume dependency). Restaurant viability

Restaurants need a minimum batch size to make Bario worth their kitchen's time. The key ratio is bars per restaurant — with 5 restaurants per bar and only 3 bars per restaurant, each restaurant receives fewer than 2 orders per slot at base assumptions. The viable minimum is 6+ orders per slot, requiring either: fewer restaurants per bar (3, not 5), or more bars per restaurant (5+), or significantly higher guest volume per bar.

The path forward
Phased roadmap
4–8 weeks · 2–3 bars · 1 restaurant · zero tech
Do things that don't scale. Validate assumptions before building anything. Use WhatsApp and a Typeform — not a product.
What to test
Will bars actively promote the cards?
+
Bars have zero financial incentive right now. Watch whether staff actually put out menu cards and mention Bario to guests — or ignore it. This tells you whether the passive bar model is viable or whether a revenue share is essential from day one.
Will guests scan, choose a slot, and wait?
+
The time slot model creates a hard abandonment cliff — someone who arrives at 19:15 just missed the slot. Track what percentage of scans result in completed orders. Track what happens to guests who miss a slot. If abandonment is high, the rolling slot model (order by :45 for the next hour) needs to be tested immediately.
Can the restaurant hit a fixed slot reliably?
+
On-time delivery is existential. A late delivery on a Friday night poisons the bar relationship and the guest experience simultaneously. Track every delivery to the minute. If a restaurant misses more than 20% of slots in the first two weeks, replace them — this is an indicator of structural misfit, not bad luck.

Key decisions
Raise the commission to €3.50+
Critical
+
At €2.00/guest, you need ~3,900 venues for €1M MRR. You will only know the commission ceiling by testing it. Frame it to restaurants as guaranteed demand, not just commission. A restaurant earning €600/month reliably from Bario needs a concrete reason to resist a commission increase — test whether they have one.
Give bars a revenue share
Critical
+
Even €0.50/meal changes bar staff behaviour from passive to active. Staff who earn something from promoting Bario will mention it to guests, keep cards visible, and handle distribution reliably. This is not a cost — it's the cheapest customer success investment available.

Important
+

06
The competitive threat
Building defensibility against Thuisbezorgd & Uber Eats

If Bario works visibly at scale, Thuisbezorgd will notice. They have restaurant relationships, a consumer app, and delivery infrastructure. Adding a "deliver to bars" feature is a product sprint for them. The question is not whether they'll try — it's what makes Bario the better choice even after they do.

The five moats — strength rated
Venue exclusivity contracts 95/100
Sign bars to 12-month exclusivity agreements in exchange for a reduced SaaS fee. Every month you delay signing exclusivity is a month a competitor can poach. Bars pay €29/month with exclusivity vs €79 without — the discount is your moat investment.
Demand-side bar-occasion data 90/100
After 12 months and 10,000+ orders, Bario knows what food people order when they're already drinking, at which venues, on which nights, in which slots. No platform has this data — it only exists because Bario operates at the intersection of bar and food. Compounds with every new venue and every new order.
Restaurant slot exclusivity 65/100
Offer restaurants preferred-partner status in exchange for Bario slot exclusivity. Leverage is the guaranteed demand volume Bario provides. Weakens if platforms offer better economics — must be paired with demand data and volume commitments.
Operational depth — batching model 60/100
The Bario model (batched orders, fixed slots, multi-restaurant rotation, no individual delivery address) is alien to platform infrastructure. Building this is a product rewrite for them, not a feature toggle. Time-limited moat — given 12–18 months, any platform can build it.
POS integration — switching cost 55/100
A Bario integration embedded in a bar's till system creates switching costs no contract can match. A bar operator who manages food orders through their existing POS has no reason to switch to a platform requiring a separate system. Phase 2 investment.
Bar operator community & brand 35/100
Being the platform that genuinely understands bar operators. Run bar owner events, publish venue revenue benchmarks, create a community. Soft moat on its own — hard when combined with exclusivity contracts and SaaS switching costs.

Only 17% of nightlife businesses have a digital loyalty programme — yet regular customers spend 38% more. The gap is real but the reasons bars don't run loyalty are structural, not lazy.

Why bars don't do loyalty
Drinks are anonymous. No natural moment to identify a guest across a three-hour visit with five rounds ordered.
Owners are time-poor. Configuring, training, promoting and analysing a loyalty programme requires marketing capability most bar owners don't have.
Rewards don't transfer. "Free drink after 10 visits" creates margin problems and liability questions that coffee stamps don't.
Social loyalty already exists. Regulars return because they know the bartender — not because of points. A card can feel transactional and undermine that warmth.
What Bario already solves
The identity problem is solved. Food ordering requires a name (and optionally phone number). This is the identity anchor loyalty needs — without asking guests to download anything.
The touchpoint exists. The order confirmation moment is a natural place to enrol guests in a programme ("order food 3x, your 4th is free") entirely within the Bario flow.
The hard part is done. Bario doesn't need to build a loyalty platform — it needs to add a visit counter and a trigger within an existing ordering flow. The complexity is already solved.
The opportunity is real but bounded. Bario can offer food loyalty immediately (within the order flow). Full bar loyalty — including drink spend — only becomes possible once the POS integration is live. Build food loyalty in Phase 1; build bar-wide loyalty in Phase 2 once tab data is available.
